# "Are You a Heretic?" — Quiz Design Document

## Quiz Philosophy

The quiz uses **progressive reveal**: each question presents a genuine theological choice, and after the user answers, the site immediately reveals which council, confession, or authority condemned (or would condemn) that view, who historically held it, and what happened to them. The cumulative effect — question after question, every answer triggers a condemnation — builds the thesis: *everyone is a heretic according to someone*.

## Tone Progression
- **Questions 1-5:** Playful, accessible, slightly cheeky. Hook the user.
- **Questions 6-12:** Smarter, more historically rich. Start teaching.
- **Questions 13-18:** Genuinely thought-provoking. The user realizes the pattern.
- **Final summary:** The rhetorical payoff. Reflective, warm, and challenging.

## THE QUESTIONS

### Q1: The Trinity Trap (Modalism vs. Tritheism vs. Anti-Nicene Councils)
**Question:** "How would you describe the relationship between Father, Son, and Holy Spirit?"
- A) "They're one God who appears in three different modes or roles"
- B) "Three persons who share one divine nature — equal, eternal, inseparable"
- C) "The Father is supreme; the Son is divine but subordinate to the Father"
- D) "I'm honestly not sure what the Trinity is supposed to mean"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Modalism/Sabellianism.** Condemned since the 3rd century (Tertullian, Hippolytus). One God switching between modes — not three eternal persons. Oneness Pentecostals hold this explicitly. Condemned at Constantinople (381).
- B → **The Nicene-Constantinopolitan position (325/381).** Sounds safe? It's not. This formula — "three persons, one substance" — was **condemned by more 4th-century councils than affirmed it**. The Councils of Antioch (341), Arles (353), Milan (355), Sirmium (351, 357, 359), Rimini (359), Seleucia (359), and Constantinople (360) all rejected the key term *homoousios* ("one substance"). For 20 years (360–380), this was officially heretical in the Roman Empire. Jerome wrote: "The whole world groaned and was astonished to find itself Arian." You agree with Nicaea and Constantinople I, but at least 10 councils between them say you're wrong.
- C → **Subordinationism / Semi-Arianism.** This was the *majority* position in the 4th century — held by the homoiousian ("similar substance") and homoian ("just like") factions. Affirmed at the Councils of Antioch (341), Sirmium (351–359), Rimini (359), and Constantinople (360). But condemned at Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381), which declared the Son fully co-equal. Also condemned by the Athanasian Creed: "none is greater or less than another."
- D → **Agnosticism on the Trinity.** Honest, but every creedal tradition from 325 AD onward requires Trinitarian affirmation. The Athanasian Creed literally opens with: "Whosoever will be saved, before all things it is necessary that he hold the Catholic Faith. Which Faith except every one do keep whole and undefiled, without doubt he shall perish everlastingly."

### Q2: Who Is Jesus? (Arianism vs. Nicene vs. Anti-Nicene Spectrum)
**Question:** "Was there ever a 'time' when Jesus didn't exist?"
- A) "Yes — God created Jesus before everything else, as the first and greatest creation"
- B) "No — Jesus has existed eternally, of the same substance as the Father"
- C) "Jesus became divine — he was a human who was adopted/elevated by God"
- D) "Jesus is divine but not equal to the Father — the Father is greater"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Arianism.** The big one. Condemned at the Council of Nicaea (325 AD). Arius was exiled. The Nicene Creed was literally written to exclude this view with "begotten not made, of one substance with the Father." Jehovah's Witnesses hold a modern version.
- B → **The Nicene position — but don't relax.** This is what Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381) affirmed. But for most of the 4th century, this was the *losing* side. The phrase "of one substance" (*homoousios*) was rejected by the Councils of Antioch (341), Sirmium (351, 357), Rimini (359 — 400 bishops coerced into reversing their pro-Nicene vote), and Constantinople (360), which made the rejection empire-wide for 20 years. Athanasius, the champion of this view, was exiled *five times*. Pope Liberius was exiled and tortured. The "orthodox" view spent most of the century as a persecuted minority.
- C → **Adoptionism.** Condemned in the 2nd-3rd centuries. Theodotus of Byzantium was excommunicated by Pope Victor I (~190 AD). Also condemned at the Council of Frankfurt (794 AD). But Adoptionism keeps recurring — the idea that Jesus "became" God's Son at his baptism or resurrection has deep biblical roots (Romans 1:4, Acts 2:36).
- D → **Subordinationism / Homoianism.** This was actually the *official position of the Roman Empire* from 360–380 AD. The Council of Constantinople (360) declared the Son is "like" (homoios) the Father but rejected the idea of shared substance. The homoiousians at the Council of Ancyra (358) said "similar substance" — close but not equal. Both were condemned at Constantinople (381), but for 20 years they *were* the orthodoxy. You'd be in the majority in 4th-century Christianity but condemned by the council that eventually won.

### Q3: Did Jesus Really Suffer? (Docetism vs. Full Humanity)
**Question:** "When Jesus wept at Lazarus's tomb, what was happening?"
- A) "God in human form was demonstrating compassion as an example for us"
- B) "A genuine human being was genuinely grieving the death of his friend"
- C) "Both — truly God AND truly human, experiencing real human emotion"
- D) "He was probably performing grief to connect with the people around him"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Docetist tendencies.** "Demonstrating" implies performance, not genuine experience. If Jesus was *demonstrating* rather than *experiencing*, his humanity isn't real. Condemned since the earliest church (1 John 4:2-3 is likely anti-Docetist).
- B → **Possibly Nestorian.** If you emphasized "a human being" so much that the divinity drops away, Ephesus (431 AD) would like a word. Nestorius was deposed and exiled.
- C → **The Chalcedonian position (451 AD).** Two natures, one person — that's what the council settled on. But the Oriental Orthodox churches rejected Chalcedon entirely, and the *how* of this formula is where all the remaining Christological heresies live...
- D → **Docetism.** Condemned from the earliest period. If Jesus performed grief rather than felt it, his humanity is a costume. The Gnostics loved this idea.

### Q4: The Cross (Atonement Theory)
**Question:** "What did Jesus's death on the cross actually accomplish?"
- A) "He paid the penalty for our sins — taking the punishment we deserved so God's justice is satisfied"
- B) "He defeated the powers of evil, sin, and death — liberating humanity from spiritual bondage"
- C) "He demonstrated the ultimate example of self-sacrificing love, inspiring us to live the same way"
- D) "He restored the broken relationship between God and humanity — healing what was fractured"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Penal Substitutionary Atonement.** Dominant in Reformed theology since Calvin. But the Eastern Orthodox Church considers this view a Western distortion that makes God sound like an angry judge who needs to punish *someone*. Orthodox theologians have called it borderline heretical.
- B → **Christus Victor.** The *earliest* atonement theory (Irenaeus, 2nd century). This was the dominant view for 1,000 years. But post-Reformation Protestants often dismiss it as inadequate — it doesn't explain *how* sins are forgiven.
- C → **Moral Exemplar / Moral Influence Theory.** Associated with Peter Abelard (12th century). Most evangelicals consider this dangerously insufficient — it reduces the cross to a motivational speech. The label "liberal theology" gets thrown at this view.
- D → **Recapitulation / Healing Model.** Close to Irenaeus and the Eastern Fathers. Beautiful theology, but many Western Protestants say it's too vague about the mechanism of forgiveness.

**The kicker:** The NT uses ALL of these metaphors (legal, military, exemplary, relational) without systematizing them into one theory. Every "theory" of atonement is a partial truth that someone else considers insufficient or dangerous.

### Q5: Free Will and Salvation (Pelagian Spectrum)
**Question:** "How does someone become a Christian?"
- A) "They hear the gospel and choose to accept it — it's a personal decision"
- B) "God chose them before the foundation of the world — they respond because God made them willing"
- C) "God offers grace to everyone, but each person must cooperate with that grace"
- D) "People are basically good and can find God through sincerely seeking truth and living rightly"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Semi-Pelagianism.** Condemned at the Council of Orange (529 AD). If the human "decides" first and God responds, you've made human will the initiator of salvation. 84% of evangelicals talk this way. Orange says grace must come first.
- B → **Calvinism / Augustinianism.** Affirmed at the Council of Orange (529) and by Reformed confessions. But the Arminians, most Catholics, most Eastern Orthodox, most Methodists, and most Pentecostals say this makes God the author of evil (he chose NOT to save some). Condemned by Arminians as fatalism.
- C → **Synergism.** Catholic and Orthodox position. But strict Calvinists say this is just dressed-up Semi-Pelagianism. Dort (1618) condemned it.
- D → **Pelagianism.** Condemned at the Council of Carthage (418 AD), reaffirmed at Ephesus (431 AD). Pelagius was excommunicated. This is perhaps the most widely held "heresy" in popular Western culture — "be a good person and you'll be fine."

### Q6: The Bible's Authority
**Question:** "Where does authoritative Christian teaching come from?"
- A) "The Bible alone — it's the only ultimate authority for faith and practice"
- B) "The Bible interpreted through the church's tradition and teaching authority"
- C) "The Bible, tradition, reason, AND personal experience all play a role"
- D) "The Holy Spirit speaks directly to believers today with authority equal to Scripture"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Sola Scriptura.** Core Protestant principle. But the Council of Trent (1545-1563) formally anathematized this position. Catholics say you can't interpret the Bible properly apart from the church that compiled it.
- B → **Catholic/Orthodox position.** Protestants say this elevates human tradition to divine authority and was exactly the problem the Reformation addressed. Luther called it "the Babylonian captivity of the church."
- C → **The Wesleyan Quadrilateral.** John Wesley's method. Calvinists say this opens the door to liberalism by giving "reason" and "experience" authority alongside Scripture. Traditionalists on all sides are suspicious.
- D → **Montanism.** Condemned ~177 AD. Montanus claimed the Holy Spirit spoke new revelation through him and prophetesses Priscilla and Maximilla. The early church shut this down.

### Q7: What About the Old Testament?
**Question:** "How do you relate to the God of the Old Testament?"
- A) "The OT God seems harsh and wrathful — I much prefer the loving God Jesus revealed"
- B) "Same God, but the OT was a different era with different rules — we've moved past that"
- C) "The OT and NT reveal the same God — wrath and love are both present in both testaments"
- D) "I honestly don't read the Old Testament much — the New Testament is what matters for Christians"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Marcionism.** Marcion was expelled from Rome in 144 AD for teaching that the OT God was a different, inferior deity. You've softened it, but the impulse is the same: the OT God is embarrassing.
- B → **Dispensationalism / Supersessionism.** The idea that God operates in different "dispensations" has complex theological baggage. Taken too far, it functionally discards OT ethics. Many theologians call this "functional Marcionism."
- C → **The most widely affirmed position** across traditions — Catholic, Reformed, Orthodox, evangelical. But affirming it is the easy part. It requires grappling with genuinely difficult texts (conquest of Canaan, divine violence, etc.) rather than explaining them away. And even within this "consensus," the disagreements on *how* to reconcile the two testaments are enormous.
- D → **Functional Marcionism.** You haven't rejected the OT in theory, but you've abandoned it in practice. Marcion would approve.

### Q8: Images, Art, and Worship
**Question:** "Is it appropriate to have paintings or statues of Jesus in a church?"
- A) "No — that's dangerously close to idolatry. God said 'no graven images'"
- B) "Yes — images help us worship and remind us of sacred truth"
- C) "The cross is fine, but realistic depictions of Jesus feel wrong"
- D) "It doesn't matter either way — it's just art"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Iconoclasm.** Condemned at the Second Council of Nicaea (787 AD). The iconoclasts lost. Most Protestants are functional iconoclasts and don't realize an ecumenical council ruled against them.
- B → **The Second Council of Nicaea's position (787 AD)** — affirmed by Catholics and Eastern Orthodox. But if you're Protestant, many Reformed confessions (Heidelberg Catechism Q96-98, Westminster Larger Catechism Q109) call religious images a violation of the second commandment. So the council that approved this is the same council most Protestants quietly ignore.
- C → **A middle position** that satisfies almost nobody. Catholics say you're too restrictive. Strict Reformed say you're too permissive.
- D → **Adiaphora** (a matter of indifference). Some traditions accept this. Others say nothing in worship is "indifferent" — the Puritans and the Regulative Principle crowd would disagree strongly.

### Q9: The Lord's Supper
**Question:** "When you take communion, what's happening with the bread and wine?"
- A) "They literally become the body and blood of Christ"
- B) "Christ is spiritually present in and through the elements"
- C) "It's a memorial — we're remembering what Jesus did, and the bread and wine are symbols"
- D) "I'm not sure, but something meaningful and sacred is happening"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Transubstantiation.** Catholic doctrine, defined at Lateran IV (1215) and reaffirmed at Trent. All Protestants reject this. Luther said the bread remains bread. Calvin said the presence is spiritual. Zwingli said it's symbolic.
- B → **Calvin's "real spiritual presence"** or **Luther's "sacramental union."** Catholics say this doesn't go far enough. Baptists and Zwinglians say it's unnecessarily mystical.
- C → **Zwinglianism / Memorialism.** Most Baptists and many evangelicals hold this. But Luther called Zwingli a heretic to his face at the Marburg Colloquy (1529). Catholics consider this a grave error. The Orthodox call it a departure from 2,000 years of church teaching.
- D → **Honest but doctrinally insufficient** according to virtually everyone with a confessional position.

### Q10: Baptism
**Question:** "Who should be baptized, and how?"
- A) "Infants of believing parents — baptism replaces circumcision as the covenant sign"
- B) "Only believers who can profess their own faith — dunked fully under water"
- C) "Baptism is important but the method and timing aren't that critical"
- D) "Baptism is symbolic — it doesn't actually do anything spiritually"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Paedobaptism.** Catholic, Orthodox, Lutheran, Reformed, Anglican, Methodist practice. But Baptists, Anabaptists, and most Pentecostals say this has no biblical basis and cheapens baptism.
- B → **Believer's baptism / Credobaptism.** Baptist, Anabaptist, Pentecostal practice. But the 1529 Diet of Speyer sentenced Anabaptists to death for this view. Catholics, Orthodox, and most mainline Protestants say you're ignoring 1,500 years of church practice.
- C → **Latitudinarianism.** This tolerance is itself suspect to confessional traditions. If baptism is a sacrament (Catholic/Orthodox/Lutheran), getting it wrong is serious.
- D → **Zwinglian extreme.** Even Zwingli didn't go this far. Most traditions — even those that call baptism an "ordinance" rather than a "sacrament" — insist it carries spiritual significance.

### Q11: The End Times
**Question:** "What happens at the end of the world?"
- A) "Jesus returns, there's a literal 1,000-year kingdom on earth, then the final judgment"
- B) "We're living in the 'millennium' now — Christ reigns through the church, and he'll return for final judgment"
- C) "Most of Revelation was fulfilled in the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 AD"
- D) "The details are unclear — what matters is that God wins in the end"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Premillennialism.** Held by early church fathers (Papias, Irenaeus, Justin Martyr). But the Augsburg Confession (1530) explicitly condemned it as "Jewish opinions." Augustine rejected it. It was revived in the 19th century and is now dominant in American evangelicalism.
- B → **Amillennialism.** Augustine's view. Dominant in Catholic, Orthodox, and Reformed traditions for over 1,000 years. But premillennialists say you're "spiritualizing away" clear biblical prophecy.
- C → **Preterism.** A minority view that some call heretical (especially "full preterism" which says ALL prophecy is fulfilled, including the second coming). The Second Coming hasn't happened yet — or has it?
- D → **Pan-millennialism** ("it'll all pan out in the end"). Theologically lazy according to every systematic theologian, but probably the most common actual belief among churchgoers.

### Q12: Hell
**Question:** "What is hell?"
- A) "Eternal conscious torment — the unsaved suffer forever in separation from God"
- B) "Destruction — the unsaved eventually cease to exist"
- C) "A temporary state of purification before eventual restoration"
- D) "A metaphor for the consequences of rejecting God's love in this life"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Eternal Conscious Torment.** The "traditional" view. But annihilationists (including John Stott, one of the most respected evangelicals of the 20th century) called it morally monstrous and biblically dubious.
- B → **Annihilationism / Conditional Immortality.** Condemned by the Fifth Lateran Council (1513). Most Protestant confessions reject it. But it's growing rapidly among evangelicals.
- C → **Universalism / Apokatastasis.** Origen held this. It was condemned at the Second Council of Constantinople (553 AD). But many early fathers (Gregory of Nyssa, Clement of Alexandria) leaned this way, and some modern Orthodox theologians (David Bentley Hart) argue passionately for it.
- D → **Liberal theology / Metaphorical interpretation.** Conservative evangelicals call this a denial of biblical authority. But C.S. Lewis described hell in ways that are closer to this than to literal fire.

### Q13: Can You Lose Your Salvation?
**Question:** "Once someone is genuinely saved, can they lose their salvation?"
- A) "No — once saved, always saved. God's grip doesn't slip."
- B) "Yes — the Bible warns about falling away, and people can reject the faith"
- C) "It's complicated — genuinely saved people won't ultimately fall away, but some who appear saved weren't really saved"
- D) "The question itself is wrong — salvation is a journey, not a one-time event"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Perseverance of the Saints** (Calvinism). Affirmed at Dort (1618). But Arminians, Catholics, and Orthodox all say the NT warnings about apostasy are real, not hypothetical. Hebrews 6:4-6 is hard to explain away.
- B → **Arminian position.** Condemned at the Synod of Dort. Calvinists say this makes salvation dependent on human performance rather than God's faithfulness.
- C → **The "no true Scotsman" approach.** Common among moderate Calvinists. Arminians say this is logically circular: "if they fell away, they were never saved; if they're saved, they can't fall away."
- D → **Orthodox/Catholic perspective.** But evangelicals call this a denial of the assurance of salvation — one of the Reformation's key pastoral gifts.

### Q14: Predestination
**Question:** "Did God choose in advance who would be saved?"
- A) "Yes — God elected specific individuals before creation, and his choice is unconditional"
- B) "God chose those he foreknew would freely choose him"
- C) "God wants everyone to be saved and gives everyone genuine opportunity"
- D) "Predestination is about God's plan for groups/nations, not individual salvation"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Unconditional Election** (Calvinism). Affirmed at Dort, Westminster. But taken to its logical conclusion, this means God also chose NOT to save specific people — **double predestination**. The Arminians, Catholics, and Orthodox call this monstrous.
- B → **Arminianism / Conditional Election.** Condemned at Dort (1618). Calvinists say this makes God's choice dependent on human action, which undermines sovereignty.
- C → **Universal atonement / Unlimited grace.** Closer to Arminian/Wesleyan/Catholic views. But Dort's "Limited Atonement" (the L in TULIP) says Christ died only for the elect.
- D → **Corporate Election.** A view associated with some N.T. Wright-influenced scholars. Both Calvinists and Arminians have criticized it for dodging the individual question.

### Q15: Church Authority
**Question:** "Who has the final say on what Christians should believe?"
- A) "The Pope, guided by the Holy Spirit, when speaking on matters of faith and morals"
- B) "The Bible, interpreted by each believer with the Spirit's guidance"
- C) "Ecumenical councils representing the whole church"
- D) "There is no single final authority — Christians must work it out in community"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Papal Infallibility.** Defined at Vatican I (1870). Every Orthodox, Protestant, and Anglican Christian rejects this. And it wasn't even Catholic dogma for the first 1,870 years of Christianity.
- B → **Radical Sola Scriptura / Soul Liberty.** Baptist distinctive. But Catholics point out this has produced 45,000+ denominations. Luther and Calvin never intended "everyone interprets for themselves." The Reformers still believed in creeds and confessions.
- C → **Conciliarism / Orthodox position.** But which councils count? Catholics accept 21. Orthodox accept 7. Some Protestants accept 4. Anglicans accept... it depends who you ask.
- D → **Honest but structurally untenable** according to every organized church. Someone always ends up being "more equal than others."

### Q16: The Holy Spirit
**Question:** "Can God give you a direct message or prophecy today?"
- A) "Yes — the Spirit speaks to believers directly, and modern prophecy is real"
- B) "God guides through Scripture and providence, but the age of direct prophecy is over"
- C) "God might impress something on your heart, but it doesn't carry the same authority as Scripture"
- D) "The Spirit speaks through the church's teaching and sacraments, not to individuals directly"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Continuationism / Neo-Montanism.** John MacArthur's Strange Fire conference (2013) called this dangerous, borderline heretical, and blamed it for prosperity gospel, false prophecy, and spiritual abuse. The early church condemned Montanus for similar claims (~177 AD).
- B → **Cessationism.** But Pentecostals and Charismatics (now the fastest-growing segment of global Christianity — over 600 million) say you've "quenched the Spirit" (1 Thess 5:19).
- C → **Soft continuationism.** A popular middle ground. But it's not really a coherent position: either God speaks or he doesn't. Hard cessationists and hard continuationists both find this unsatisfying.
- D → **High church sacramentalism.** Catholic and Orthodox emphasis. But evangelicals say this locks the Holy Spirit inside an institution.

### Q17: Who Can Be Saved?
**Question:** "Can someone who has never heard of Jesus be saved?"
- A) "No — conscious faith in Jesus Christ is the only way to be saved"
- B) "Possibly — God can save through Christ even those who don't know his name"
- C) "Yes — all sincere seekers of truth in any religion can find God"
- D) "Everyone will eventually be saved — God's love wins in the end"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Exclusivism.** The dominant evangelical position. But inclusivists point to Matthew 25 (sheep and goats surprised to learn they served Christ), Romans 2:14-16 (Gentiles who follow the law "by nature"), and the fate of OT saints who didn't know Jesus's name.
- B → **Inclusivism.** Held by C.S. Lewis (arguably), Karl Rahner, many Catholic theologians. But evangelicals say this undercuts missions and contradicts Acts 4:12 ("no other name under heaven").
- C → **Pluralism.** John Hick's position. Rejected by virtually every major Christian tradition — Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, Reformed, evangelical. But it raises the genuine question: is a just God going to condemn billions of people for being born in the wrong geography?
- D → **Universalism.** Condemned at Constantinople II (553 AD). But Gregory of Nyssa (a Cappadocian Father who helped define the Trinity!) held this view. David Bentley Hart argues it's the only morally coherent position.

### Q18: Jesus and Gender/Authority
**Question:** "Within the Trinity, does the Son eternally submit to the Father?"
- A) "Yes — the Son's submission to the Father is an eternal feature of their relationship"
- B) "Only during the incarnation — in eternity, Father, Son, and Spirit are fully co-equal with no hierarchy"
- C) "I've never thought about this"
- D) "The Father has a kind of primacy — he's the 'source' — but this isn't about superiority"

**Reveals:**
- A → **Eternal Functional Subordination (EFS).** The ETS declared this doesn't conform to their doctrinal basis (2016). Critics (Kevin Giles, Millard Erickson) call it a revival of Arianism/subordinationism. The Nicene tradition insists on full co-equality.
- B → **The classical Nicene-Constantinopolitan position** as read by most historical theologians. But EFS proponents (Wayne Grudem, Bruce Ware) say you've lost the biblical pattern of the Father's authority and the Son's glad obedience — and with it, the theological grounding for complementarianism. So even agreeing with the creeds doesn't end the argument.
- C → **Most Christians' honest answer.** But ignorance of this debate doesn't exempt you from it — the implications ripple into gender roles, church leadership, and marriage.
- D → **Monarchy of the Father.** An Eastern Orthodox emphasis. The Father is the "source" (the Spirit proceeds from the Father). But Western theology says this risks subordinationism if not carefully stated.

**NOTE ON COUNCIL COUNTING:** The old design only counted 7 ecumenical councils. The updated design includes the anti-Nicene councils (Antioch 341, Arles 353, Milan 355, Sirmium councils, Rimini 359, Seleucia 359, Constantinople 360) in the condemnation tally. This means EVERY answer to Q1 and Q2 triggers condemnations from multiple councils — there is no longer any "safe" Trinitarian answer. The total council count should reflect ALL councils that would condemn the user's specific combination of views, including anti-Nicene ones.

### The Payoff Message
After the profile, the site delivers the key message:

> **Everyone who took this quiz got condemned by someone.**
>
> That's not a bug — it's the point.
>
> Every answer on every question has been officially condemned as heretical by some council, confession, synod, or authoritative body in Christian history. There is no combination of answers that escapes condemnation entirely.
>
> The words "heretical" and "orthodox" don't describe fixed truths handed down from heaven. They describe the *consensus of a particular community at a particular time and place.* The belief that "there are three persons in one God" — the most basic Christian doctrine — was officially condemned by the majority of 4th-century councils and was heretical across the Roman Empire for 20 years. What's orthodox in Constantinople in 381 AD is heretical in Constantinople in 360 AD. What's heretical in Orange in 529 is the default assumption in most churches today.
>
> **So what do we do with this?**
>
> Maybe we stop aiming to be "orthodox" — as if that's a stable target — and instead pursue what the New Testament authors actually cared about most:
>
> - **Love** — the greatest of these (1 Cor 13:13)
> - **Justice** — for the vulnerable, the oppressed, the forgotten (James 1:27, Matt 25:31-46)
> - **Unity** — not uniformity of doctrine, but unity of purpose and love (John 17:21)
> - **Humility** — including intellectual humility about our own certainty (Phil 2:3)
> - **The resurrection** — the one claim that actually distinguishes Christianity (1 Cor 15:14)
>
> The NT authors never used the word "orthodox." They never defined homoousios. They never voted on whether Christ had one will or two. They told stories, wrote letters, sang hymns, and urged their communities to love each other across the deepest divisions of their world.
>
> Maybe that's enough to start with.

## DEEP DIVE PAGES

After the quiz, each heresy links to a deeper exploration page with:
1. **The historical story** — who was involved, what happened, why it mattered at the time
2. **The actual council documents** — key quotes from the canons/anathemas
3. **Who holds this view today** — modern denominations and movements
4. **The strongest case FOR and AGAINST** — presented fairly
5. **What the NT actually says** — relevant passages, noting where the NT is silent or ambiguous on the specific formulation the council used
