# Biblical Unitarian Audit Report
## "Are You a Heretic?" Website — Full Content Audit

**Date:** 2026-03-24
**Perspective:** Biblical Unitarian (God is one person — the Father; Jesus is the Messiah and Son of God but not God himself; the Holy Spirit is God's power/presence)
**Scope:** All ~20 heresy pages and ~8 articles
**Areas audited:** Scriptural accuracy, theological bias, historical accuracy, factual errors

---

## Executive Summary

The site is **historically well-researched and engagingly written**. Most dates, names, councils, and sequences of events are accurate. The writing acknowledges genuine complexity and avoids cheap dismissals of "heretical" positions. However, from a Biblical Unitarian perspective, the site has **six systematic problems** that run through nearly every Trinity- and Christology-related page:

1. **Nicene trinitarianism is treated as the default correct answer** without serious engagement with alternatives.
2. **Biblical Unitarianism is invisible** — it's never presented as a distinct, coherent position. BU views are collapsed into "Arianism" or "adoptionism."
3. **The soteriological argument ("only God can save") is presented as self-evident** without engaging the BU counter: God the Father saves *through* the Son.
4. **Subordination texts are acknowledged but never taken at face value** — John 14:28, Mark 13:32, 1 Cor 15:28, Acts 2:36 are always explained away as incarnational or functional.
5. **Extra-biblical philosophical language (homoousios, ousia, hypostasis) is treated as a necessary development** rather than a potential overreach.
6. **The site's own best historical evidence undermines its theological conclusions** — it shows councils were politically driven and used non-biblical categories, yet still treats their conclusions as authoritative.

The non-Trinity pages (salvation, sacraments, eschatology) are largely **fair and balanced** with minimal bias.

## Severity Guide

- **MAJOR** — Significant factual error, severe theological bias, or conceptual framework that excludes BU positions entirely
- **MODERATE** — Meaningful bias, incomplete engagement with alternatives, or philosophical arguments presented as scriptural ones
- **MINOR** — Small inaccuracies, slight framing issues, or missed nuances

## Part 1: Trinity & Christology Heresy Pages

### 1.1 Arianism (arianism.mdx) — MODERATE

**What's good:** The historical narrative is accurate and engaging. The page correctly identifies that Arius's concern was protecting monotheism.

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Double standard on John 14:28 | MODERATE | The page accepts "the Father is greater than I" as literal when presenting Arianism, then dismisses it as "voluntary self-emptying in the incarnation" in the case against. A BU reading takes it as straightforward: the Father *is* genuinely greater. |
| Acts 2:36 never resolved | MINOR | "God has *made* him both Lord and Christ" is acknowledged but never explained away convincingly. |
| False binary on pre-existence | MODERATE | Sets up Arianism (created from nothing) vs. Nicene (co-eternal). Misses the BU middle ground: eternally begotten/foreknown but not co-equal. |

### 1.2 Semi-Arianism (semi-arianism.mdx) — MODERATE

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Philosophical not scriptural refutation | MODERATE | The "case against" uses Athanasius's philosophical argument ("if similar in substance, they must share substance") rather than engaging with Scripture. |
| 1 Cor 8:6 tension unresolved | MODERATE | "One God, the Father... and one Lord, Jesus Christ" is never explained within a homoousios framework. How can there be "one God, the Father" if the Son shares identical essence? |
| Athanasius's own subordinationism | MINOR | Doesn't mention that Athanasius affirmed the Father as sole *arche* (source) of divinity — a position with "subordinationist flavor." |

### 1.3 Subordinationism (subordinationism.mdx) — MAJOR

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Internal contradiction on Athanasius | MAJOR | The page admits Athanasius held what "has a subordinationist flavor" but insists this doesn't imply inequality. This is self-contradictory: either subordination exists in Athanasius (undermining the Nicene claim) or it doesn't (undermining the historical claim about pre-Nicene theology). |
| Torch analogy fails | MODERATE | "A fire that lights another torch is not 'greater' than the second fire" — but John 5:26 says the Father *granted* the Son to have life in himself. The second fire depends on the first. The analogy obscures real asymmetry. |
| Mark 13:32 unaddressed | MODERATE | The Son not knowing the hour isn't explained by incarnational humility. It's a genuine epistemic gap that the page glosses over. |

### 1.4 Adoptionism (adoptionism.mdx) — MAJOR

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| False binary | MAJOR | Sets up: either Jesus was adopted at baptism/resurrection OR he had eternal pre-existence as God. BU offers a third option: the Son existed with/in the Father's plan before creation, is begotten but not co-equal. This middle ground is absent. |
| Romans 1:3-4 oversimplified | MINOR | *horisthentos* more commonly means "marked out/defined" than "appointed." The page overstates how cleanly this supports adoptionism. |
| Hebrews 1:2-3 misapplied | MINOR | "Radiance" (*apaugasma*) doesn't necessarily mean "fully God" — it means reflection or derived glory. This distinction is not explored. |

### 1.5 Modalism (modalism.mdx) — MINOR

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Baptism narrative assumed trinitarian | MINOR | The page reads Matthew 3:16-17 as three simultaneous divine persons. A BU reads: the Father (the one God), Jesus (His human Son), and God's Spirit/power. One ultimate divine person acting, not three. |
| John 17:5 treated as settled | MODERATE | "Glory shared before the world existed" — BU scholars debate whether this refers to pre-incarnate existence or to glory in God's eternal counsel. The page treats the Nicene reading as obvious. |

### 1.6 Nicene Trinitarianism (nicene-trinitarianism.mdx) — MAJOR

This is one of the most problematic pages from a BU perspective.

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| "Only God can save" presented without counter | MAJOR | The page never engages the BU argument: God the Father can save *through* the exalted Son without the Son being God himself. The mechanism is presented as self-evident when it's precisely what's in dispute. |
| Conflates Arianism with BU | MAJOR | Uses "creature" dismissively. BU doesn't say the Son is a "creature" in the ordinary sense — he's begotten of the Father, exalted, worthy of honor. The page equates "not God" with "mere creature." |
| "Worship = idolatry" begs the question | MAJOR | Assumes honoring/worshipping the Son as God's appointed Messiah and agent would be idolatry. But this is precisely what early Christians did before Nicaea. The page treats this as obviously heretical without justification. |
| homoousios problems acknowledged but brushed aside | MODERATE | The page correctly notes the word was non-biblical, used by a condemned heretic, and "in its most natural Greek reading implied modalism" — then presents the Cappadocian rescue as sufficient. |

### 1.7 Anomoeanism (anomoeanism.mdx) — MODERATE

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Nicene response admitted as "ad hoc" but accepted | MODERATE | The page notes Aetius saw the relational/essential distinction as ad hoc, then treats it as having resolved the problem. A BU would agree with Aetius: begottenness vs. unbegottenness IS a real, essential difference. |
| High-Christology texts assumed as baseline | MAJOR | The page assumes texts like John 1:1 should be the interpretive lens for subordination texts, not vice versa. This is never defended — just assumed. A BU argues the baseline is monotheism (1 Cor 8:6) and subordination texts are literal. |

### 1.8 Homoianism (homoianism.mdx) — MAJOR

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| False binary: homoousios or vagueness | MAJOR | The page frames it as: either identical substance or meaningless "likeness." BU offers a third option: the Son is genuinely divine (derived, dependent), eternally begotten, worthy of worship — but personally subordinate to the Father. Neither "vaguely like" nor "identical." |
| Anti-creedal sentiment dismissed as naiveté | MINOR | "Can't we just stick to what the Bible says?" is treated as functionally homoian rather than a legitimate theological methodology (sola scriptura applied to Christology). |
| Political victory framed as theological | MODERATE | The page admits orthodoxy won through "imperial regime change and a generation of work" but doesn't follow this to its logical conclusion: why should politically-driven outcomes be treated as divine truth? |

## Part 2: Christological Heresy Pages

### 2.1 Docetism (docetism.mdx) — MINOR-MODERATE

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Pre-incarnate Logos assumed | MODERATE | Treats "the divine Logos" as established fact rather than debatable. A BU reads John 1:14 as God's word/plan becoming incarnate in a human, not a pre-existing divine person becoming flesh. |

**What's good:** The historical account of docetism is accurate. The critique (denying real incarnation) works from a BU perspective too.

### 2.2 Apollinarianism (apollinarianism.mdx) — MAJOR

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Entire framework presupposes divine Logos | MAJOR | The discussion of "divine mind replacing human mind" only makes sense if you accept a pre-incarnate divine consciousness. A BU says: Jesus is one person with one fully human mind, conceived by God's power. |
| Gregory of Nazianzus used selectively | MODERATE | "What has not been assumed has not been healed" actually *supports* BU — Jesus needed to be fully human. The page uses it to defend trinitarian Christology, but the principle is neutral. |

### 2.3 Nestorianism (nestorianism.mdx) — MODERATE

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Communicatio idiomatum unchallenged | MODERATE | "God died on the cross" is presented as necessarily true. A BU asks: why can't we say "the Son of God, who is human, died"? |
| Nestorius possibly caricatured | MINOR | The page acknowledges modern scholars argue Church of the East Christologies are "well within Chalcedon" — suggesting Nestorius may not have taught what he's accused of. |

### 2.4 Eutychianism (eutychianism.mdx) — MINOR-MODERATE

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Two-natures framework assumed | MODERATE | Presupposes "divine nature" and "human nature" as eternal realities requiring balance. A BU says there's no eternal divine nature in Christ to balance — he's a human being exalted by God. |
| Luke 2:52 implications not drawn out | MINOR | Jesus "learning and growing" supports the BU view (genuinely human, not eternally divine) but the page doesn't explore this. |

### 2.5 Monothelitism (monothelitism.mdx) — MINOR-MODERATE

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Assumes Aristotelian metaphysics | MODERATE | "The will belongs to nature rather than person" is philosophical, not biblical. A BU says: Jesus has one will because he's one person (human). |
| Gethsemane reading helpful but incomplete | MINOR | "Not my will but yours" is correctly read as showing genuine dialogue — supports BU view that Father and Son are distinct persons with the Son subordinate. |

### 2.6 Marcionism (marcionism.mdx) — MODERATE

**Issues:**

| Issue | Severity | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Isaiah 45:7 unexplained | MODERATE | The page quotes Marcion citing "I create evil" but doesn't actually explain how to read this. Just asserts OT God = NT God without exegesis. |
| Ironic tension with trinitarian Logos theology | MODERATE | If the eternal divine Logos can't genuinely become human (docetism's problem), trinitarian Christology may actually *create* the two-God problem Marcion tried to solve. A BU reading (God truly acts through a human Son) unifies OT and NT more cleanly. |

## Part 6: Recommendations

### For accuracy and fairness, the site should:

1. **Acknowledge Biblical Unitarianism as a distinct position** — Currently, BU views are collapsed into Arianism or adoptionism. The site should note that BU holds: the Father is the one God, the Son is God's Messiah and agent (exalted, worthy of honor, but not God himself), and the Spirit is God's power/presence.

2. **Engage the BU soteriological counter-argument** — "Only God can save" is presented as self-evident. The site should at least note the counter: God the Father saves *through* the faithful Son (Heb 10:12, Acts 4:12, 1 Tim 2:5).

3. **Take subordination texts at face value in the "case for"** — John 14:28, Mark 13:32, 1 Cor 15:28, and Acts 2:36 deserve to be presented as potentially literal, not automatically explained away.

4. **Apply its own skepticism consistently** — The site's best insight is that councils were politically driven and used non-biblical categories. It should follow this insight to its conclusion rather than treating Nicene outcomes as authoritative.

5. **Note the anti-Nicene period page erases Unitarianism** — The "both sides" framing (Nicene vs. anti-Nicene) is a false binary. There was always a third option: strict monotheism with an exalted but subordinate Son.

6. **Clarify Arius's actual position** — "There was when he was not" is a hostile summary. Arius taught eternal dependence of the Son on the Father, not simple temporal creation.
